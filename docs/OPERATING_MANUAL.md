# The HomeAtlas Operating Manual

**Edition 1 · The company, as run.**

This is not the Standard. The Standard explains what HomeAtlas believes. This manual explains what HomeAtlas *does* — the repeatable procedures that turn the belief into a Tuesday. If the Standard is the constitution, this is the way the office actually opens, the trucks actually leave, and the promises actually get kept.

**Who this is for.** The person running operations day to day. If the founders were unreachable for two weeks, you should be able to run the company from this book alone — not perfectly, but faithfully. Every procedure below has a **Trigger** (what starts it), an **Owner** (the one role accountable), the **Steps**, what **Done looks like**, and a short **Never** list.

**The two things you are protecting, always.** When a procedure doesn't cover your exact situation, fall back to these:

1. **The promise.** Every membership is a set of visits we owe. Never let the number of promises quietly outrun our ability to keep them.
2. **The record.** Every home has one continuous story. Never let a visit happen without adding to it, and never let money, software, or a bad day erase it.

If an action protects both, do it. If an action protects one at the cost of the other, stop and escalate (see Part 8).

## Part 1 — The Daily Rhythm

### 1.1 Morning — before any truck leaves

**Trigger:** Start of every operating day.
**Owner:** Operations lead (you).

**Steps**

1. **Open the board.** Pull today's scheduled visits. For each one confirm three things exist: a real address, a named technician assigned, and a member in good standing. A visit missing any of the three does not leave the lot until it's fixed.
2. **Run the arrears check (5 minutes, non-negotiable).** Cross-reference today's visits against members whose most recent payment failed. Any member in arrears is **pulled or held** before the route is released — we never send a truck to perform free work we then can't discuss at the door (see 6.2). Mark each held stop with a reason.
3. **Read the previous record for each stop.** For every property on today's route, the technician gets the short brief: last visit date, who was there last, open recommendations, gate/access notes, pets, and anything flagged "watch." Nobody arrives at a home blind. Thirty seconds of history is the difference between a stranger and a steward.
4. **Confirm the day is physically possible.** Members × today's visits against crew-hours available. If the day is over-committed, you reschedule the *lowest-urgency* stops now, in the calm of the morning — not at 3 p.m. in a panic. Notify any moved member before they wonder (see 5.x cadence for tone).
5. **Set the one number.** Decide the single thing that matters today (e.g., "every west-facing hard-water home gets a before/after photo"). Say it once. Don't drown it in ten priorities.

**Done looks like:** Every truck leaves with a route where each stop has a paid member, a named tech, and a read history. You know which stops were held and why. You know today's one number.

**Never:** release a route with an unresolved arrears stop; send a technician to a property whose record they haven't seen; move a member's visit without telling them.

### 1.2 During the day — the running promise

**Trigger:** Continuous, while visits are underway.
**Owner:** Operations lead.

Keep a light hand. The field does the work; your job is to remove friction and catch the two failures that matter: a visit that won't happen (a truck stuck, a no-access) and a visit that happens but leaves no memory. When a visit can't be completed, it is **rescheduled, not dropped** — the obligation survives; only the date moves. Log the reason on the property record ("no access — dog loose, rebooked").

### 1.3 After every completed visit — the close-out

**Trigger:** A technician marks a visit complete.
**Owner:** The technician, verified by ops at day's end.

A visit is not finished when the glass is clean. It is finished when the home knows more than it did this morning. Every completed visit leaves **at least one** of: an observation, a photo, a recommendation, or a health note. "We finished" is not a close-out. "Here's what we learned" is.

**Steps (the technician, in under two minutes, at the property)**

1. Record what was done — the checklist, in the member's language.
2. Add the memory: at minimum one observation *or* one photo *or* one recommendation *or* one health note. If a west window is developing hard water, that sentence is worth more than the invoice.
3. Separate the audiences. Anything for the homeowner's eyes (preferences, what we noticed, what we recommend) goes to the member record. Anything internal (a code, a crew note) stays internal. When unsure, it's internal.
4. Confirm the promise ledger ticks: this visit counts against what the member is owed for the year.

**Done looks like:** The member could open their portal tonight and see a new chapter — a date, what was done, maybe a photo, maybe a note — with no fake data and no accounting. After signing, run the **Founder Acceptance Test** (`docs/operations/FOUNDER_ACCEPTANCE_TEST.md`) before sharing the portal link; see also `wargames/011-customer-1-launch-checklist.md`.

**Never:** close a visit with zero memory added; expose an internal note to the member; invent a health score or a photo that wasn't taken.

### 1.4 End of day — reconcile

**Trigger:** Trucks are back.
**Owner:** Operations lead.

1. Every scheduled visit is now one of: **completed with memory**, **rescheduled with a reason**, or **flagged** (something went wrong — see the relevant procedure).
2. Any completed visit missing its memory gets chased tonight, not next week. A memory recalled the next morning is already half-lost.
3. Any member who was held for arrears gets moved into the payment procedure (6.2).
4. Tomorrow's route gets its first glance so the morning starts ahead, not behind.

**Done looks like:** No visit is in an unknown state. Every promise kept today is written down. Every promise moved is dated and explained.

## Part 2 — The Weekly Rhythm (every Friday)

**Trigger:** End of the operating week.
**Owner:** Operations lead; founders informed.

Friday is where drift gets caught while it's still cheap. Review, in order:

1. **Promises owed vs. kept.** For the week: visits promised, visits delivered, visits rescheduled, and the running annual balance per member. This is the single most important number in the company — a membership business dies not from losing customers but from quietly owing more than it can deliver. If the gap is growing, you are overselling capacity. Stop taking new members before you fix it.
2. **The held list.** Every member pulled for arrears this week — resolved or still open? Nobody should sit in limbo. Each becomes either paid-and-rescheduled or moved to a documented pause/cancel path.
3. **Memory completeness.** What share of this week's completed visits left a real observation, photo, or recommendation? A falling number is the archive developing holes — the moat leaking. Name the crews or days where it slipped and why.
4. **Recommendations made vs. resolved.** What did we notice this week that a member should decide on? Nothing urgent should sit unspoken. Nothing declined should be re-pitched — it gets *remembered respectfully* and revisited only with new evidence (a photo showing it got worse).
5. **The week's one honest failure.** Name it plainly — a missed window, a late arrival, a note that leaked, a member who felt forgotten. Write it down. The company that records its mistakes is the company that stops repeating them.

**Done looks like:** You can answer, on Friday, "Are we keeping our promises, and is the record getting richer or thinner?" — with numbers, not a feeling.

**Never:** end a Friday with an unexplained gap between promises made and promises kept; carry an unresolved held member into a new week without a decision.

## Part 3 — The Monthly Rhythm

**Trigger:** First operating day of the month (this is also the billing day — see 6.1).
**Owner:** Operations lead + whoever holds the money.

1. **Billing runs and reconciles.** Every member scheduled for service this month is billed on the 1st (6.1). By end of day, three columns agree: who we billed, who paid, and who is scheduled. Any name in one column but not the others is a task, not a mystery.
2. **Cash, not just revenue.** Money arrives in waves (a member billed quarterly pays four times a year; the launch cohort may all bill in the same months) while costs — labor, materials, fuel — are continuous. Look at cash-in, cash-out, and the balance. A company can be "healthy" on annual revenue and out of cash in the same month; only this number tells you.
3. **The forward look.** Next month's expected billing is derivable from next month's schedule. Know the number before the month starts.
4. **Retention.** Of the members who joined twelve months ago, how many are still here? This is the number that decides whether the whole model works. Watch the trend, not the month.
5. **Margin sanity on the flagship tier.** The premium tier includes complimentary treatments every visit. Those have a real cost. Confirm, at least monthly, that the best-selling tier still makes money after that cost. "Trust-first pricing" must not quietly become "unprofitable pricing."
6. **Founding cohort.** Confirm the first members still carry their "member since" designation. It cost nothing to record and can never be granted retroactively — protect it.

**Done looks like:** Billing reconciled to three agreeing columns; a real cash-position number; a retention trend you could show a founder without flinching.

**Never:** treat "collected revenue" and "cash on hand" as the same number; discover the flagship tier's margin for the first time at year-end.

## Part 4 — Member Lifecycle

### 4.1 After a member signs

**Trigger:** A membership is signed and a card is on file.
**Owner:** A single named person — the **activation owner**. This is the most important ownership assignment in the company. If it belongs to "whoever notices," it belongs to no one, and the proudest moment becomes the first broken promise.

**Steps**

1. **Book the first visit within a defined window** (set it — e.g., N business days) and put it on the schedule. A signed member with no visit on a calendar is not really a member yet; they are a promise with no date.
2. **Log the signing as the first entry** in the property's record — the relationship's chapter one, dated.
3. **Fill the silence.** Between signing and the first visit is an empty stretch where a premium brand goes mute and a new member starts to wonder if they made a mistake. A short, warm "here's what happens next" closes it. Not marketing — reassurance.
4. **Track it on one shared list** with two columns: *signed date* and *first-visit-booked date*. Every signed member appears here until booked. An empty gap on this list is the company's single highest-risk failure; work it to zero daily.

**Done looks like:** Every new member has a first visit on the calendar and a first entry in their record, and heard from us before they had to ask.

**Never:** let a signed member fall into the gap between "signed in our system" and "booked on the route"; let the activation list carry an unbooked member overnight without a reason.

### 4.2 When a payment fails

**Trigger:** A scheduled charge is declined.
**Owner:** Operations lead (billing).

**Steps**

1. **Catch it before the truck, not after.** A failed charge on the 1st and a visit scheduled for the 15th must connect — the member is held from dispatch until resolved (this is the morning arrears check, 1.1).
2. **Reach out kindly and early.** A failed card is where a premium relationship is either made or broken. The tone is "your card didn't go through — here's an easy way to fix it," never a threat. One graceless message undoes ten perfect visits.
3. **Apply the consequence honestly.** Member benefits (including add-on discounts) are suspended while payment is unresolved, and reinstated the moment it clears — but only apply what the agreement actually promises, and never charge an amount that contradicts what the member signed.
4. **Resolve within the week.** By Friday, the member is paid-and-rescheduled or moved to a documented pause or cancellation. No one lives in arrears limbo.

**Done looks like:** No in-arrears member receives a free visit; every failed payment is either resolved or converted to a pause/cancel with dignity, within the week.

**Never:** send a technician to collect payment at the door; let a failed payment silently ride while service continues; use a punitive tone.

### 4.3 When someone pauses

**Trigger:** A member asks to pause (travel, a lean season, a snowbird winter).
**Owner:** Operations lead.

A pause is a relationship on hold, not a fight. Treat it as respectfully as a signing.

**Steps**

1. Record the pause as an event on the property record, with a reason ("paused for winter travel") and an intended return. History, not deletion.
2. Stop billing for the paused period; stop scheduling for it. The obligation ledger adjusts — we don't owe visits for months we're not serving, and we don't bill for them.
3. Keep the record warm. A paused member's story stays intact and waiting; nothing is erased.
4. Set a gentle return touchpoint near the intended date — an invitation, never a nag.

**Done looks like:** Billing and scheduling both stopped for the paused window, the pause is on the record with a reason, and the door is visibly open to return.

**Never:** treat a pause as a cancellation; keep billing a paused member; delete anything.

### 4.4 When someone cancels

**Trigger:** A member cancels.
**Owner:** Operations lead.

Luxury brands are defined by their exits. A cancellation handled with grace becomes a future referral; handled badly it becomes a story they tell.

**Steps**

1. **Honor the terms exactly** as signed (the agreement provides for cancellation with thirty days' written notice). Confirm the effective date in writing.
2. **Settle the ledger cleanly.** Any visits already owed and paid are either delivered or fairly reconciled. No surprises, no clawbacks framed as penalties.
3. **The record stays theirs.** Their home's history is not held hostage against cancellation — it remains available to them. Making leaving easy is precisely why so few do.
4. **Close warmly.** "Your home's record is yours to keep, and we'd be honored to resume anytime." Then stop. No retention gauntlet.

**Done looks like:** A clean, dated close; the member keeps their record; the last impression is generosity, not friction.

**Never:** hold a record hostage to prevent a cancellation; add a retention obstacle course; let the final interaction be about money.

### 4.5 When a customer sells their home

**Trigger:** A member is selling, or has sold, a property under care.
**Owner:** Operations lead.

This is the moment the company's deepest belief gets tested: the home, not the owner, is the permanent thing. The record was built for exactly this.

**Steps**

1. **Preserve the record with the home.** The property's history does not vanish because the owner changed. It is anchored to the home and continues across the ownership change — mark the new era, keep the old one.
2. **Offer the seller their record to take.** They own their history; they can carry it (and, where they wish, present it at sale — a home with a documented care history is worth more than one without).
3. **Introduce continuity to the new owner** where appropriate: the home already has a story and a steward, and they can inherit that continuity rather than starting from zero.
4. **Handle the membership honestly** — the seller's agreement follows the agreement's terms; the new owner is offered their own relationship, never silently inherited into a contract they didn't sign.

**Done looks like:** The home's record survives the sale intact; the seller leaves with their history; the new owner is met, not assumed.

**Never:** let a home's twenty-year record reset because a name on the deed changed; auto-enroll a new owner into the previous owner's agreement.

## Part 5 — Field & Weather

### 5.1 When a technician notices something important

**Trigger:** During any visit, a technician sees something that matters — worsening hard water, a failing gutter seam, a cracked pane, an access hazard, damage that wasn't there last time.

**Owner:** The technician captures it; operations routes it.

The technician is the company's eyes. What they notice is the entire reason the record has value. An observation that stays in a technician's head is a memory the company never had.

**Steps**

1. **Capture it as observation, not diagnosis.** "West gutter seam separating — watched since spring, worse today, photo attached." Grounded in what was seen, dated, ideally photographed. Never a guess dressed as a fact.
2. **Classify the urgency.** *Watch* (note it, revisit next visit), *Recommend* (the member should decide — route it to them plainly, explained, never as an upsell ambush), or *Act now* (a safety or damage issue — flag to ops today).
3. **Route by urgency.** Watch → the record. Recommend → the member, with the reasoning. Act now → operations same day, and the member is told before they discover it themselves ("we noticed X today; here's what we suggest").
4. **If we caused it or missed it, say so first.** Confession before discovery — "we missed the garage transom, we're returning Tuesday, no charge" — is the most trust-building thing the company can do. It requires an honest incident note on the record.

**Done looks like:** The observation is on the record, classified, and — if it needs a human decision — in front of the right person with the reasoning attached.

**Never:** let a real observation go unrecorded; present a guess as an observation; turn a recommendation into a hard sell; hide something we broke or missed.

### 5.2 When rain starts

**Trigger:** Weather disrupts the day — rain, a storm, hail.

**Owner:** Operations lead.

This company works in the weather; the weather is not an edge case, it's a Tuesday. The goal is that the member's experience of being cared for survives the disruption.

**Steps — rain during the operating day**

1. **Reschedule, don't cancel.** A rained-out visit is a moved date, not a lost obligation. The promise ledger is untouched.
2. **Reflow calmly.** Move the affected stops as a set; don't let one rainy Tuesday cascade into three broken weeks. Rebook across a month boundary carefully — a visit that slips into a different month is also a billing question (6.1); flag it.
3. **Tell the member first.** "Today's rain means we're moving your visit to Thursday — nothing you need to do." Proactive beats apologetic. Silence is the only wrong move.

**Steps — after a storm at a member's home**

4. **Reach out, unprompted.** "Last night's hail hit your area — want us to take a look?" This is the purest expression of the whole company: someone is watching over the home. It costs a message and returns more trust than any feature.
5. **Document any storm damage** on the record, dated and photographed — it may matter to the member's insurer, and it is exactly the kind of memory the home should keep.

**Done looks like:** No obligation lost to weather; members hear from us before they wonder; storms become moments of care, not gaps in service.

**Never:** drop a rained-out visit instead of rescheduling it; let a member discover the reschedule themselves; ignore a storm that hit a member's home.

## Part 6 — Money

### 6.1 The billing rhythm

**Trigger:** The 1st of each month.
**Owner:** Whoever holds the money (with the operations lead).

The rule, memorized: **members are billed on the first day of the month in which their scheduled service will be performed, and payment is never collected in the field.** The visit happens later that same month, so when the technician arrives there is nothing to pay. Money moves on a fixed rhythm and disappears from the doorstep, so the visit can be about the home.

**Steps**

1. On the 1st, bill every member scheduled for service this month.
2. Reconcile to three agreeing columns: billed, paid, scheduled (Part 3).
3. Any decline immediately enters the payment-failure procedure (4.2).
4. A visit rescheduled across a month boundary changes which month it bills in — catch it, don't let billing and the calendar disagree.

**Done looks like:** Everyone serviced this month was billed on the 1st; no technician ever discussed money at a door; the three columns agree.

**Never:** collect payment in the field; bill for a month you won't serve; let a reschedule silently desync billing from the schedule.

### 6.2 The arrears truck

**Trigger:** A member with a failed payment has a visit on today's route.
**Owner:** Operations lead (this is the morning arrears check, 1.1).

Because we bill before we serve and never collect at the door, a failed payment plus a scheduled visit is a trap: the truck rolls, the work is done for free, and the technician — correctly — can't even mention it. Close the trap before dispatch: cross-reference failed payments against today's stops and **hold** the affected visits until payment resolves. Whether we ever knowingly serve an in-arrears member is a founder-level policy; absent one, the default is hold-and-resolve.

### 6.3 Disputes and chargebacks

**Trigger:** A member disputes a charge ("I never authorized this," "you didn't show," "I cancelled").
**Owner:** Operations lead.

Billing a stored card with no one present guarantees occasional disputes. Winning a legitimate one requires assembling three proofs on a deadline: **authorization** (the signed agreement), **the charge** (the payment record), and **delivery** (the completed visit with its memory). Keep a one-page playbook naming where each proof lives and who assembles it — decided before the first dispute, not during it. Watch the dispute rate itself: a rising rate can cost the company its ability to bill at all, so it belongs in the Friday review the moment it moves.

## Part 7 — The Lines That Never Move

These are not procedures; they are the edges of the procedures. When a situation isn't covered above, do not cross these to make a day easier. They are why members choose us and why they stay.

- **No fabricated anything.** Never show a member a score, a photo, a visit, or a history that didn't happen. An honest empty state ("your story starts with your first visit") beats a convincing fiction every time. The day we ship a guess as a fact is the day the record stops being worth anything.
- **The record only grows.** We supersede; we don't erase. A replaced screen, a former owner, a completed restoration — all stay as history. Deleting anything requires deliberate intention and a reason, never convenience.
- **Care in front, accounting behind.** A member sees their home's story — visits, photos, what we noticed, what's next. They do not see "amount invested," lifetime spend, or business metrics. The relationship is always more visible than the bill.
- **The promise is a ledger, not a vibe.** We always know how many visits we owe, to whom, by when. Growth that outruns that number isn't growth; it's the quiet beginning of a subscription that can't deliver.
- **Consistency over speed, always.** Members leave companies for inconsistency, not for slowness. When the two conflict, protect the consistent experience.
- **The record is held in trust.** The home's history belongs to the home and its people; we are its keeper, not its owner. It is theirs to take, and protecting it (codes, photos, access notes are sensitive) is as sacred as building it.
- **Every hire holds the promise.** The newest technician carries the company's most important surface — the actual visit, the actual homeowner at the door. No one works a solo visit before they've ridden along, seen how a record is read and written, and understood why the memory matters. Culture is transmitted, or it dilutes one unbriefed stranger at a time.

## Part 8 — Running HomeAtlas for Two Weeks Without the Founders

If the founders are unreachable, the company does not stop and does not improvise its identity. It runs this book.

**What you own and decide freely (reversible):** the daily route, reschedules, weather reflow, member communications in the established tone, holding an in-arrears stop, booking first visits, capturing and routing observations, honoring a pause or a cancellation on stated terms.

**What you keep steady but do not change (foundational):** pricing, membership structure and terms, what the tiers include, the billing rhythm, the standards in Part 7. You *apply* these; you do not *revise* them. If reality seems to demand a change, that's a founder decision — document the case and wait.

**What you escalate rather than decide:** anything that would break a Part 7 line to survive a hard day; any incident touching a member's safety, data, or a broken promise you can't cover; the flagship tier turning unprofitable; the promise ledger gap widening faster than you can close it; the first real dispute that threatens the ability to bill. Escalation is not failure — it's the system working. Write the situation down, protect the promise and the record in the meantime, and hold.

**The one test for any decision this book doesn't cover:** *Does this help us care for this home more consistently than we could without it, and does it keep a promise we already made?* If yes, proceed. If no, don't — and if you can't tell, protect the promise and the record, and escalate.

**Daily continuity check (60 seconds, every morning):** Are all of today's promises staffed and paid? Did every visit yesterday leave a memory? Is anyone stuck in arrears limbo or the activation gap? If those four are green, the company is being run faithfully, founders present or not.

## How this manual stays alive

This is Edition 1. It is meant to be corrected by reality, the same way the company itself is. When a procedure here fails a real day — a step that didn't fit, an owner that was unclear, a case nobody wrote down — capture what actually happened and what you did, and fold it back in. A procedure that only ever describes the calm day is a procedure that breaks on the hard one. The company that records its operations is the company that can hand itself to a stranger for two weeks and still be itself when it comes back.

*Nothing in this manual is a feature or a piece of software. It is the company — the set of repeatable things we do so that no home in our care ever has to start over.*
