# WARGAME 009 — The Constitution Test

*A completeness audit of THE HOMEATLAS STANDARD, read as a constitution rather than a spec. Written from the vantage of twenty years on, as wargames 005 and 008 were — but turned inward, on the document itself, not the market. The question is not "what should we build?" It is "if the founder were gone, would this document still know what HomeAtlas believes?"*

**Discipline of this document.** No features. No roadmap items. No product redesigns. No fiction. No expansion of the company. Every finding cites text already in the corpus — the Standard, VISION, the Apollo Directive, the roadmap, or wargames 001–008. Where the Standard is silent, I say *silent*; I do not fill the silence with invention. The output is a set of **promotion candidates** for the Standard's own five-step process (build → notice → name → test → promote); per the Promise and the process notes, nothing here becomes Standard text automatically. It is step-zero material: named, tested against the corpus, offered up.

**One honesty note before the audit.** Two documents the corpus treats as canonical are *referenced everywhere and present nowhere* in the repository: `STANDARD_PROCESS_NOTES.md` (the five-step promotion process — cited by wargames 004 and 008) and the "clarifications doc" (the A1–C2 clarifications — cited by wargames 007 and 008). Wargame 004 states plainly that the Standard "lives outside this repo (founder's session outputs)." That fact is itself the first constitutional finding, and it recurs below: **the Standard's own rules of self-government are not inside the Standard.** I have audited what is present and marked, rather than guessed at, what is absent.

The seven sections below are the seven questions posed to this audit, answered in order, and then the single verdict.

---

## 1 — MISSING PRINCIPLES

*Not features. Not engineering. Places where a future founder would genuinely not know what HomeAtlas believes.*

The philosophy is remarkably complete on **why the company exists** (Volumes I–II) and **how the product should behave** (Volume III). The gaps are not in those. They are at the edges the founder never had to adjudicate because he was in the room. Six beliefs are load-bearing in practice and unwritten in principle.

**1.1 — The constitutional status of the Record.** The Standard preserves memory obsessively — Law 002, Law 003, "collect once, use forever" (3.1), "memory only grows" (3.3), "Property Memory belongs to the property itself, not merely the customer" (3.4.4). But it never states *who owns the record, and what rights the homeowner holds over it.* Can a homeowner take it? Delete it? Withhold consent to it? The corpus's own aspiration — the transferable Passport read at closings (wargame 005), "your record is yours" (wargame 004 §11.5), export as a "sacred right" (wargame 008 §2) — depends entirely on a belief the Standard has not written down. Wargames 004/005/008 all converge on the phrase **"memory is held in trust — HomeAtlas is custodian, never owner,"** and all three call it a promotion candidate. It is *the* missing principle: everything about the record's twenty-year value rests on an ownership doctrine the constitution omits.

**1.2 — The duty to protect memory.** "Held in trust" has two halves, and the Standard has neither. Beyond preservation, trusteeship means *protection* — and the archive is, viewed coldly, "a burglary-intelligence database" of interior photos, gate codes, and alarm notes for affluent homes (wargame 004 §13.4; the entire security campaign of wargame 001). The Standard has operational hygiene ("never expose internal notes," 3.4.4) but no *principle* of data trust: consent to photograph, classification of secrets vs. memories, the duty to guard the archive, breach-readiness. A future founder facing a photo-consent question or a breach would find the constitution silent on the one asset it spent twenty years accumulating. Protection is not a feature; it is the unstated second half of stewardship.

**1.3 — The limit of persuasion.** The Standard is emphatic that the product must not manipulate — no fabricated data (Law 001), no dark patterns (3.4.7), "trust compounds; manipulation does not" (3.4.5), pricing "does not exist to maximize revenue" (3.4.5), "optimize for earning confidence, not extracting value" (Parking Lot). The belief clearly exists in fragments. What is missing is the *consolidated principle governing the seam between stewardship and selling*: when does a recommendation become an upsell? Wargame 003 §8 ("a concierge's quiet suggestion, never an upsell ambush") and wargame 004 §5 ("declined recommendations remembered respectfully, not repeated as a pitch") both improvise an answer the Standard never states. A steward who also sells needs a written line, or the line moves under revenue pressure.

**1.4 — What the company owes the people who do the work.** The technician is "the primary sensor of the platform" (3.1) — but described as an *input*. The Standard protects the technician's time and attention fiercely (3.4.6) yet says nothing about what the company owes the technician *as a person*: fair treatment, the dignity of the craft, recognition, the work being seen. Volume II Ch. 6 ("Builders, Not Employees") gestures at it and stops. Wargame 005 elevates exactly this — "the sensor is a person," "The Guild," work that is "seen, remembered, and signed" — and wargame 007 §9 shows the failure already latent: no way the philosophy reaches the first hire. This is load-bearing: if good technicians are not retained, the memory stops being trustworthy at its source. A labor principle is not an HR nicety; it is the provenance guarantee for the entire Record.

**1.5 — The philosophy of endings.** The Standard is a document about beginnings and continuity. It has a product rule that "pausing and cancelling are as respectful as signing" (3.4.8) — but no *belief* about how relationships end: the right to leave, the graceful exit, death, the record that survives the company (wargame 005's Escrow). Wargame 004 §13.3 names it precisely: "a stewardship company that hasn't designed its endings hasn't finished designing stewardship." A constitution that only knows how things begin cannot guide the half of stewardship that is letting go.

**1.6 — How the Standard governs itself.** The Promise says "if this document ever becomes a barrier to serving homeowners well, improve the document." Volume II Ch. 7 says foundational decisions "change slowly, deliberately, rarely." But the actual *procedure* — who may amend the Standard, by what threshold, through what steps — lives in `STANDARD_PROCESS_NOTES.md`, outside the constitution and absent from this corpus. A constitution that does not contain its own amendment clause is, by definition, incomplete. This is the meta-gap that Sections 5, 6, and 7 all return to.

## 2 — INTERNAL TENSION

*Principles that pull against one another. The instruction is to clarify them, not resolve them. Each below is a real pair, both sides quoted, with the question the founder never had to answer written plainly.*

**2.1 — Memory vs. Privacy.** *"Memory never expires... Deleting history should require intention"* (3.4.4); *"superseded, never deleted... memory only grows"* (3.3) — against the homeowner's control over their own data, which sometimes *requires* deletion (a homeowner who wants interior photos gone; a right-to-be-forgotten regime; wargame 004 §13.4). The Standard's deepest instinct — never forget — collides directly with a person's right to be forgotten. The corpus even logs the collision as an open question: *"How should privacy work across ownership changes?"* (3.4.2). **The clarification the founder owes his heirs:** memory-permanence serves *the home*; privacy serves *the person*; when they conflict, which outranks — and does "never delete" mean *never*, or *never for our own convenience*?

**2.2 — Growth vs. Curation.** *"Curated growth. We add companies when the platform can make them better — not when we need ARR"* (VISION) and *"growth... remains a consequence, not the mission"* (Vol II Ch. 11) — against the VISION document's own ten-year arc, which *commits* to becoming a multi-company platform (years 3–4) and to "category definition" (years 9–10), an outcome that requires scale. Wargames 006 §Campaign 2 and 008 §3 sharpen it: the refusal to grow is the moat, and it also caps the platform. This tension runs *between two canonical documents* — the Standard prizes the refusal; the Vision commits to the scale. **The clarification owed:** is HomeAtlas willing to stay small forever to stay pure, and if not, what is the exchange rate between curation and reach?

**2.3 — Founder Judgment vs. Systems That Outlast the Founder.** *"If Noah would not use the feature on tomorrow's job, do not build it"* (Law 006); *"culture cannot be delegated. It is demonstrated"* (Vol II Ch. 5) — against *"built as though the company intends to exist for generations"* (Vol II Ch. 9) and *"no engineer permanently owns HomeAtlas... a system intended to outlast them"* (4.9). The Standard simultaneously anchors its quality bar to one living person *and* insists the company must outlive him. This is the deepest tension in the corpus, and wargame 008 §7 names it. **The clarification owed:** is "Noah" a person or a role? When he is gone, what stands where the "would Noah" test stood? (This is the hinge of Section 5.)

**2.4 — Reality-First vs. the Twenty-Year Vision.** *"Reality always outranks vision"* (Founding Rules); *"build from reality outward. Never from imagination inward"* (Vol II Ch. 3); Law 006 (usage earns implementation, not ideas) — against the Apollo Directive, which is pure aspiration and whose closing "future vision" quote is *itself a prediction* ("I noticed your west-facing windows have developed minor mineral spotting... I recommend scheduling within three weeks"), and against a Vision that commits a decade ahead. Wargame 008 §5 notes the tension "already lives inside the corpus." The Standard partly guards itself here — Volume VII insists "the future is an aspiration, not a prediction" — but the Apollo Directive does not observe that discipline. **The clarification owed:** how does a reality-first company hold a twenty-year vision without letting the vision quietly overrule reality — and does the Apollo Directive's aspirational voice sit *under* the Founding Rule, or beside it?

**2.5 — Calm/Simplicity vs. the Completeness of Memory.** Law 007 (Simplicity Wins), Law 009 (*"luxury software whispers"*), Vol II Ch. 8 — against Law 002 (Memory Comes First), "every observation has future value" (Principle 005), a record that by design *accumulates for a century*. Memory adds; simplicity subtracts. A twenty-year home record is a vast and growing thing, and keeping its surface calm while its contents compound endlessly is a permanent squeeze. The corpus logs it as an open question — *"How should memory age visually?"* (3.4.4). **The clarification owed:** when completeness of the record and calm of the interface genuinely conflict, which law yields to which?

**2.6 — "Every Visit Must Add Memory" vs. "Protect the Technician's Two Minutes."** Law 003 (*"a visit that leaves no memory is an incomplete visit"*) and "every visit must add memory" (3.4.4) — against 3.4.6's hard constraint that the workflow "finish in under two minutes," "typing is expensive," protect the technician's attention. Richer memory demands more capture; respect for the technician demands less. The Parking Lot already names the collision: *"Every click in the Technician Experience competes with caring for the home."* **The clarification owed:** the memory mandate and the attention mandate constrain the same two minutes at the doorstep — which one gives, and by what rule?

**2.7 — "The homeowner outranks the software" vs. "the home outranks the feature."** The Promise ranks *homeowner > software* and *home > feature* — but never *homeowner vs. home*. And 3.4.4 says the record ultimately "belongs to the property itself, not merely the customer." So when a living person's wish conflicts with the home's permanent record (2.1 in concrete form — the owner wants deleted what the home's continuity wants kept), the Promise's own hierarchy has no tiebreaker. **The clarification owed:** insert the missing comparison — when the person and the property disagree, who wins?

## 3 — PLACES THAT WILL AGE POORLY

*Wording likely to become incorrect in five, ten, or twenty years. The instruction is to strengthen it without changing its meaning. Each item gives the current wording and a meaning-preserving rephrase — offered as candidates, not edits.*

**3.1 — Named individuals inside durable law.** *Current:* "If **Noah** would not use the feature on tomorrow's job, do not build it" (Law 006); "Would **Noah** be proud to show this to his best customer" (3.4.2). A Law that names a mortal person ages into a riddle the moment that person is gone (wargame 008 §7). *Meaning-preserving strengthening:* keep Noah as the **origin** of the test, not its eternal object — "Would the person who has to do tomorrow's job actually use this? (Law 006 began as *would Noah use it* — the test is the founder's field judgment, and it must survive the founder.)" The meaning — field-tested usage outranks ideas — is unchanged; the dependency on a living referent is removed. This is the single most consequential aging fix in the document.

**3.2 — Capability claims about AI stated as fact.** *Current:* "No AI can predict every reality of operating a home service company" (Vol II Ch. 3); "Humans notice what has not happened yet... Software cannot do any of that and should not be designed as though it eventually will" (3.1). These are phrased as *facts about what AI cannot do* — and facts about capability age, often within a single year. *Meaning-preserving strengthening:* restate them as *choices about what HomeAtlas will not do*, which do not age. "HomeAtlas does not let un-witnessed inference enter the record as fact — not because a machine could never describe a home, but because a trust business cannot afford a confident guess in someone's living room" (the exact lesson wargame 006's competitor learns the hard way). The moat is the refusal, and a refusal is a value, not a limitation of the tool. The belief survives every improvement in AI; the capability claim does not.

**3.3 — "No software. No AI. No CRM. Just philosophy."** *Current:* the framing line under "The Memory of the Home." Read literally in twenty years it is simply false — Atlas *is* AI. *Meaning-preserving strengthening:* "This began as philosophy, before any software, any AI, any CRM — and the philosophy still outranks all three." The intended meaning (philosophy precedes and governs the tools) is kept; the literal falsehood is retired.

**3.4 — Taste anchored to 2026 brands.** *Current:* "Would **Apple** ship this?" (Apollo, Every Feature Must Pass); "Build Like: Apple · Porsche · Rivian · Notion · Linear · Arc Browser" (Apollo); "References: Apple, Rivian, Porsche, Mint Window Cleaning" (3.4.7). Companies fade; a test that outsources judgment to a brand ages with the brand. *Meaning-preserving strengthening:* anchor to the *quality* the brand exemplifies, brand as illustration — "Would this meet the bar these products set in 2026: restraint, inevitability, the absence of surprise? (Apple, Porsche, Rivian, Linear — named as examples of the standard, not owners of it.)" The Apollo Directive's own datestamp ("v1.0 · July 4, 2026") already signals it is a snapshot; this makes the snapshot survive its references.

**3.5 — Vendor names inside Law-level text.** *Current:* Law 008's examples ("Pricing → `COMPANY_SETTINGS`... Google Reviews → Production Place ID"); the 3.3 source-of-truth table naming Jobber; scattered Supabase/Stripe/Resend/Vercel references. The Standard mostly handles this well — it scopes them "v0.1," "for now," and Volume IV explicitly relegates frameworks to the Handbook ("should still be true in 2055"). The residual risk is the few vendor names sitting in *durable* text rather than the living surfaces. *Meaning-preserving strengthening:* wherever a vendor appears in Law-level prose, name the *role* beside it — "Jobber (or whatever external system owns scheduling)" — so the sentence outlives the vendor. Meaning identical; brittleness removed.

**3.6 — "Locked member pricing."** *Current:* the "locked pricing" promise carried in the tiers and agreement, surfaced by wargames 004 §6.2 and 008 §4 as the one place the evidence supports *evolution*, not mere clarification. The word "locked," unqualified, will age into either a margin trap (can never raise) or a broken promise (raise badly). *Meaning-preserving strengthening — the intended meaning made explicit:* "locked against *arbitrary or opportunistic* change — not locked forever against inflation — with any increase indexed, pre-announced, and generous." This keeps the promise the founder actually meant (you will never be gouged) while removing the reading that becomes a liability under duress. Per wargame 008, this is the principle most likely, if left as written, to force the exact "one compromise at a time" failure Volume II Ch. 4 warns against.

**3.7 — "Draft 1 / Last Updated July 2026" across stable philosophy.** *Current:* nearly every surface in Volume III carries "Status: Draft 1, Last Updated: July 2026." In 2036 an unrevised "Draft 1" reads as *neglected* rather than *deliberately stable*. The Standard already has the right instinct — the Vol III format note distinguishes "literary and durable" (I–II) from "operational and living" (III–VII). *Meaning-preserving strengthening:* extend that labeling to a status vocabulary that separates *"stable — rarely revised by design"* from *"living — expected to change,"* so a future reader does not mistake settled philosophy for an abandoned draft.

## 4 — HIDDEN ASSUMPTIONS

*Beliefs the Standard depends on but never states. Three are load-bearing in the strict sense the brief asks for: removing the assumption collapses multiple sections at once. They are marked ◆.*

**◆ 4.1 — The customer is an affluent, long-tenure, owner-occupied, single-family home.** Unstated, and underneath almost everything. Memory compounds *over years of one owner* (Vol I; 3.4.4). The record "sells the house twice" (wargame 005) assumes the home is sold intact with one clear owner. "Where children learned to walk" (Vol I Ch. 7) assumes emotional owner-occupancy. Premium pricing assumes affluence. Remove this assumption — renters, institutional single-family-rental owners, high-churn properties, multi-decision-maker households, the property manager of twelve homes (wargame 004 §2.4, §6.4; 008 §6) — and *four sections weaken together*: the homeowner-psychology chapters, the memory-compounding thesis, the pricing philosophy, and the entire twenty-year moat. This is the deepest hidden assumption in the corpus, and the Standard never once says "we assume this home."

**◆ 4.2 — Human physical labor at the property stays central and economical.** "The technician remains the primary sensor of the platform" (3.1) — permanently. This bets that skilled human labor visiting homes remains economically viable for premium recurring service. Remove it — wage inflation and the trades-labor shortage (wargame 008 §4), or a change in the physical work itself — and the "technician as sensor" architecture that *sources* the entire Record, and grounds Laws 001, 003, and 011, loses its origin. "Nothing enters the record that a person did not witness" (wargame 005) is only a guarantee while a person is economically present to witness. The Standard never states that it is betting on the durability of human field labor.

**◆ 4.3 — The company stays independent enough to hold its own refusals.** The refusal-is-the-moat thesis (wargame 006) and "growth is a consequence, not the mission" (Vol II Ch. 11) both assume HomeAtlas can *choose* to refuse. That silently assumes no investor, acquirer, or board can override the Standard — yet wargame 008 §1 warns that "outside capital eventually forces the choice under duress, at the worst possible time, on someone else's terms." The Standard is entirely silent on ownership, capital, and governance. Remove the assumption of independence and *every refusal in the document becomes unenforceable at once* — which is why this belongs with 4.1 and 4.2 as load-bearing. A constitution that can be overruled from outside is advisory, not binding, and the Standard never secures the ground it stands on.

**4.4 — Trust is scarce, decisive, and premium-payable.** Vol I ("The Product Is Trust") treats it as self-evident that trust differentiates *and* that enough customers will pay more for it to sustain the model. Both are market bets (wargame 008 §10, the commoditize-from-below / bundle-from-above squeeze), not axioms.

**4.5 — Customers actually want to be remembered.** The whole edifice assumes homeowners *want* a relationship, want to be "known," want a twenty-year record — rather than wanting the job done cheaply and forgotten. Some genuinely prefer transactional anonymity; wargame 006 §Campaign 1 notes the day-one customer "cannot tell the difference and hasn't read their constitution." The Standard never entertains that its central value might be a minority taste.

**4.6 — The founder's taste is correct, transferable, and durable.** The Standard is the encoding of one person's judgment as the quality bar. It assumes that taste is right, teachable, and still right in twenty years. If it is wrong or dates, the document faithfully preserves the error. Vol II Ch. 5 frames founder-primacy as strength; it never names it as an assumption that could fail.

**4.7 — One jurisdiction, one currency, one culture.** Dollars, California auto-renewal law, "Chico." The universality of "care for homes" quietly rests on a single legal and cultural context; "home" and "stewardship" are not culture-neutral. Minor beside 4.1–4.3, but real the moment the map extends past California.

## 5 — THE FUTURE FOUNDER TEST

*Assume Noah is gone. A founder inherits HomeAtlas in 2046. Every place below is where, reading only the corpus, she would honestly say: "I don't know what Noah would have wanted."*

1. **The decision procedure itself.** Law 006 and the quality checklists resolve to "would Noah." With Noah gone, the heir cannot run the test the constitution tells her to run (wargame 008 §7). The document's court of final appeal is a person who no longer exists.
2. **When a refusal may bend.** The moat is the refusal (wargame 006), and every refusal is tested under duress (wargame 008). The Standard lists refusals but gives no threshold and no defense procedure. Facing margin or competitive duress, the heir cannot tell whether "we will not build generic field-service software" (Law 012) was meant as *absolute* or as a *strong default that survival could override.*
3. **Deleting a record.** A homeowner (or a regulator) demands interior photos be erased. "Memory never expires" (3.4.4) collides with the demand, and the heir cannot tell whether Noah meant *never* or *never for our convenience* (§2.1). The clarification the corpus claims exists (B6) is in a document not present here.
4. **"Locked pricing" in an inflationary year.** Forever, or against arbitrary change? (§3.6.) Getting it wrong is a margin crisis or a trust betrayal, and Noah never wrote down which he meant.
5. **How big is too big.** The Vision commits to platform scale; the Standard calls growth a consequence. The heir cannot tell Noah's real limit — curated boutique forever, or category platform — when the two genuinely conflict (§2.2).
6. **Whether to serve the non-ideal customer.** The property manager, the renter, the institutional owner, the unsigned spouse (wargame 004 §2.4, §6.4). The Standard assumes the ideal customer (§4.1) and never rules on the edges, so the heir cannot tell whether Noah would say "serve them" or "that's not who we're for."
7. **What "premium" means once the reference brands fade.** "Would Apple ship this?" defined premium by pointing at a brand (§3.4). When the brand no longer signifies what it did in 2026, the pointer dangles and the heir cannot recover the referent.
8. **How to change the Standard.** The amendment process lives outside the document (§1.6). "Improve the document" (The Promise) does not say by whom, by what threshold, or what Noah held immutable versus improvable. The heir inherits a constitution without a stated way to amend it.
9. **What the company owes its people.** No labor principle (§1.4). Scaling to two hundred technicians, the heir cannot tell what Noah believed about pay, craft, apprenticeship, or the technician's dignity.
10. **Three of seven volumes.** Volume V (Design), Volume VI (Business — the economics that must sustain everything), and Volume VII (The Future) are undrafted; Volume VI carries a single principle. Dozens of "Open Questions" lists across 3.4 are catalogued uncertainties. The heir inherits a constitution that is, by honest admission, half outline — and cannot know what Noah would have written for the very volumes about the company's next twenty years.

The pattern under all ten: the Standard's supreme interpretive authority is a mortal person, and its amendment clause is a missing file. To the document's great credit, it *marks* most of these gaps rather than hiding them (the drafts, the open questions, the deferred lists). Honesty about a gap is a constitutional virtue. But a marked gap is still a gap, and every item above is a real decision the heir cannot make from the text alone.

## 6 — THE REFUSALS

*Every place the Standard says "we will not," explicit or implicit — then the question the brief demands: are they sufficient to preserve the company's identity under pressure?*

**The explicit refusals** are abundant and, in their domain, excellent:

- *Identity* — "The Company We Refuse to Become" (Vol II Ch. 11): no growth-at-the-expense-of-customers, no productivity-over-craft, no features-over-clarity, no automation-replacing-relationships, no employees-as-resources, no optimizing-every-metric-except-trust. And the one costly precommitment in the whole corpus: *"If protecting trust costs us speed, protect trust. If maintaining quality costs us convenience, maintain quality."*
- *Category* — "What We Will Not Become" (VISION): no generic field-service CRM, no AI-chatbot company, no race-to-the-bottom marketplace, no feature factory.
- *Craft/taste* — "Never Build" (Apollo): no generic SaaS, dashboards, spreadsheet UI, clutter, "anything that feels like software"; "Never bounce, shake, celebrate, confetti"; "Never oversell, exaggerate, marketing clichés."
- *Product ethics* — Law 001 ("HomeAtlas does not ship fiction"); Law 004 (never surface spend); Law 012 (never generic field-service software); 3.4.5 (no surge, auction, opaque, behavioral, or psychological pricing — "trust compounds; manipulation does not"); 3.4.7 (no dark patterns, countdown timers, fake scarcity); 3.4.6 (no gamification, leaderboards, streaks); Atlas "does not decide, does not promise, never invents" (3.1, 3.4.3).
- *Operations* — payment never collected at the door (Membership Billing); "Explicitly Not on Roadmap" (generic-CRM parity, native apps, cheapest-bid marketplace, public chatbot, light mode).

**The implicit refusals** — real, load-bearing, and never gathered as refusals:

- *We will not let un-witnessed inference enter the record as fact* (Laws 001/011; wargame 005's "nothing enters the record that a person did not witness" — the deepest refusal in the company, and it is nowhere stated *as* a refusal).
- *We will not grow by lowering the bar* (curated growth; "one small compromise at a time," Vol II Ch. 4).
- *We will not make leaving hard* (implied by portability, never stated as a refusal — yet wargame 006 §Campaign 3 shows this is the moat itself).
- *We will not make certain things easy* (the Parking Lot's unanswered homework: deleting history, overwriting memory, changing canonical truth, breaking a Law, publishing without review — "difficulty protects quality").

**Are they sufficient under pressure? No — and the insufficiency is specific.** The refusals are rich in *taste* and *product ethics*, and thin exactly where the twenty-year pressure is heaviest. Three structural problems:

1. **They are scattered and unranked.** The refusals live in five documents and a dozen sections. Nothing gathers them; nothing distinguishes the *costless aesthetic* refusals ("never confetti" — never tested, because keeping it costs nothing) from the *costly identity* refusals (grow slower, earn less, stay small, protect data at a price — the ones that will actually be tested). A refusal that costs nothing to keep is not a moat. The document's only refusal phrased as a costly precommitment is Vol II Ch. 11's "if protecting trust costs us speed, protect trust" — which is the correct shape, and nearly alone in it.

2. **The two refusals most needed under pressure are absent.** There is *no refusal about capital or ownership* — nothing that says "we will not take money, or sell to an owner, that forces us to abandon the Standard" — even though wargame 008 §1 identifies capital as the pressure most likely to force the choice. And there is *no refusal about the archive* — nothing that says "we will not sell, mine, or turn the record against the homeowner" — even though the archive is simultaneously the moat and a "burglary-intelligence database" (wargame 004 §13.4). "Memory is held in trust" *would be* this refusal; it is not yet in the Standard.

3. **They have no defense procedure.** Wargame 008's meta-finding, stated as sharply as the corpus states anything: the Standard describes a calm, five-step process for *adding* principles and *no process for defending them under pressure* — "which is when they actually get violated." The refusals are written in peacetime and will be tested in duress, and Volume II Ch. 4's "one compromise at a time" is precisely the mechanism by which unranked, undefended refusals fall.

Wargame 006's conclusion is the right one and this audit affirms it: *"The moat is the refusal... I can outspend their marketing. I cannot outspend their discipline."* The refusals are sufficient to defeat a competitor and to resist aesthetic drift. They are **not yet sufficient to preserve identity under duress**, because the refusals that matter most under pressure are the least explicitly written, the least ranked, and the least defended — and two of them (capital, data) are not written at all. **The single highest-value strengthening in this entire audit is to consolidate every "we will not" into one article of the Standard — each marked Law (absolute) or Guideline (strong default), the costly ones distinguished from the costless, the capital and data refusals added, and a defense clause attached: foundational decisions are held *most* firmly exactly when the market is screaming to move fast** (Vol II Ch. 7, turned from description into procedure).

## 7 — THE CONSTITUTION TEST

> *If HomeAtlas never added another principle after today, could this document still guide the company fifty years from now?*

**Answer: not yet — but the gap is structural, not philosophical, and the document is perhaps one disciplined editing pass away from yes.**

That two-part answer is the honest one, and it needs both halves.

**Why the soul is already there.** Volumes I and II operate at the altitude a constitution must — trust, memory, stewardship, consistency, the refusal to lower the bar — values the closing of Volume I rightly calls valuable "in every generation." The document consciously separates timeless *why* from temporary *how* (the Vol III format note; Volume IV's relocation of frameworks to a Handbook that "should still be true in 2055"; 4.9's "preserve HomeAtlas's way of thinking, not yesterday's technology") — which is exactly the discipline that lets a constitution outlive its materials. The Promise supplies a genuine ordering — *reality > opinion; evidence > hierarchy; homeowner > software; home > feature; mission > all* — and a ranked hierarchy is precisely the instrument that lets a constitution resolve cases its authors never imagined. And wargame 006 already demonstrated the document functioning *as* a constitution: it survives a hostile, better-funded adversary "for exactly as long as the Standard holds." A document that can be read as a threat model and win is doing constitutional work. On philosophy, the honest verdict is that the brief's own instruction — *do not invent new principles* — is correct: the principles are largely complete.

**Why the skeleton is not yet there.** Five structural facts, each established above, each disqualifying on its own at the fifty-year horizon:

1. **The final interpretive authority is a mortal person.** The document resolves hard cases with "would Noah" (Law 006, §3.1, §5.1). A constitution whose supreme court is one named living human cannot, unamended, guide past that human's life — the very mechanism it uses to decide is non-transferable as written.
2. **The amendment clause is outside the document.** The five-step promotion process lives in a file not in the constitution and not in this corpus (§1.6). A constitution that cannot state how it is legitimately changed cannot govern fifty years of change.
3. **The refusals — the actual moat — are scattered, unranked, partly implicit, undefended, and in two cases (capital, data) unwritten** (Section 6). Identity under pressure is preserved through refusals; as filed, they cannot hold across fifty years of duress.
4. **Half the constitution is an outline.** Volumes V, VI, and VII are undrafted, and Volume VI — the economics that must sustain everything else — is nearly empty (§5.10). A document cannot guide fifty years on questions three of its seven volumes have not answered.
5. **A few claims are pinned to 2026** — capability claims about AI that will age into falsehood, taste anchored to brands and to a named person (Section 3). Small beside the others, but real.

**The synthesis, which is the point of the whole audit.** What stands between this document and *yes* is not more belief. It is the **constitutional connective tissue that lets belief survive its author** — and, tellingly, nearly all of it is *already present in the corpus, in the wrong place or the wrong form*:

- a **transferable interpretive authority** to stand where "would Noah" stands (reword Law 006 and the checklists — §3.1 — the test is the founder's field judgment, and it must outlive the founder);
- an **amendment-and-defense clause inside the Standard** (relocate the five-step process *into* the document and add the missing defense procedure — §1.6, Section 6);
- a **consolidated, ranked, complete article of refusals**, capital and data included (Section 6 — the highest-value single change);
- a handful of **reframings** that turn aging capability-claims into timeless value-claims and taste-by-brand into taste-by-quality (Section 3);
- and, over time, **completion of the three outlined volumes** through the same five-step process that produced the first four.

Four of those five are acts of *consolidation, relocation, and rewording* of material the corpus already contains — not new principles. That is why the answer is "not yet" rather than "no," and why the fix is an editing pass rather than an invention. The most precise form of the verdict: **the Standard today has the philosophy of a fifty-year constitution and the governance of a founder's working document.** Give it one article — call it, in the corpus's own future-tense, *"Volume 0: How This Document Governs Itself"* — that names its successor-authority, contains its own amendment and defense procedure, and gathers its refusals into one defended list, and the answer to the Constitution Test turns from *not yet* to *yes.*

Until that article exists, the document guides the company for exactly as long as the people reading it remember what the founder meant — which wargame 006 already told us is the whole game, and wargame 008 already told us is the thing peacetime documents lose under pressure. This audit's only addition is to locate, precisely, the five places where the memory is not yet written down — so that the company built to ensure *no home ever starts over* does not, itself, start over the day its founder is gone.
